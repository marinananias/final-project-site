From Harpsichord to Hyper-Instrument

How Instrument Technology Reshaped Musical Style

This digital exhibit explores how changes in keyboard instrument technology, from the early eighteenth century to the present, influenced compositional style, expressive possibilities, and listening practices.

Project Argument

My central argument is that musical style does not develop independently. It is shaped by the physical possibilities and limitations of instruments. By tracing keyboard history from the harpsichord to digital and MIDI-based instruments, I want to show that technology influenced not only what composers wrote, but also how performers approached sound and how audiences listened.

1. Harpsichord

The project begins with the harpsichord. Because its strings are plucked rather than struck, it offers limited dynamic variation. That mechanical reality encouraged clarity of articulation, ornamentation, and texture.

In this section, I will connect those constraints to repertoire by composers such as Domenico Scarlatti, asking how style grows out of the instrument itself.

2. Fortepiano

The next section focuses on the fortepiano and its touch-sensitive dynamics. Unlike the harpsichord, this instrument allowed performers to shape phrases through changes in touch and volume.

I plan to discuss Mozart and Haydn here, especially how their keyboard writing takes advantage of contrast, nuance, and expressive phrasing that earlier instruments could not support in the same way.

3. Nineteenth-Century Piano

This section will look at the industrial development of the piano, including stronger frames, expanded range, and greater sound projection. These changes made possible a more powerful and dramatic style of writing.

I want to connect this especially to Romantic repertoire and to Franz Liszt, whose music depends on the larger sonic world of the modernizing nineteenth-century piano.

4. Twentieth Century to Present

The final historical section moves into prepared piano, electronic mediation, MIDI, and digital instruments. John Cage will serve as a bridge into twentieth-century experimentation, while later examples will help me think about how keyboard expression changes when sound becomes programmable.

Current Draft Status

This is a working draft of the project website. My next steps are to refine the written analysis, add audio examples, include visual diagrams of instrument mechanisms, and build one interactive comparison feature so users can hear how technology shapes sound directly.
